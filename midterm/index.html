TRANSACTION PROCESSING

The method by which an accounting system processed transactions of connected operations, such as the sale of goods to clients, the purchase of commodities and payment to vendors, the manufacturing of completed items for sale, and the payment to workers for services given, are known as Transaction Cycles.
We have three different types of transaction cycles: Inventory Transaction Cycle, Sales Transaction Cycle, and Payment Transaction Cycle.
The process of buying and paying for goods and services is known as the Expenditure Cycle, and it consists of a number of actions. These responsibilities include determining what has to be purchased, carrying out the actual purchase, receiving the supplies, and compensating the suppliers.
The Conversion Cycle is the process of employing cash to generate goods and services, giving them to customers, and then gathering the related receivables and turning them back into cash.
In accounting, the Revenue Cycle is a process that starts with getting a customer order or signing a contract with a customer, continues with providing an order to the client, and concludes with receiving money from the client.
A document, which may also be used to begin processing transactions, records an economic occurrence. Some publications are produced via transaction processing. We have three types of documents: Source Document, Product Document, and Turnaround Document.
A Source Document will serve as verifiable evidence that a transaction actually occurred, while the Product Document is the document that is produced as a result of the transaction. The Turnaround Document is the document that another system uses as a source document.
Every financial transaction that a company makes is completely recorded in an accounting JOURNAL. It is sometimes referred to as the "book of original entry" since it is the first place where transactions are entered. We have two types of journals: General Journal and Special Sheet.
A Special Journal is one that handles specialized transactions, such as sales or cash receipts. The use of customized journals significantly reduces the time required to record transactions and submit them to ledgers. Only transactions that occur frequently or regularly necessitate the use of special journals. The General Journal stipulated that all transactions must first be documented in a general notebook, except those recorded in specialist journals such as a cash journal, purchase journal, and so on.
A Ledger is a book or collection of accounts that records account transactions. Each account has a starting or carry-forward balance, and each transaction would be recorded as a debit or credit in different columns, as well as the ending or closing balance.
A Subsidiary Ledger subsidiary ledger is a collection of comparable accounts whose total balances equal the total balance of a given general ledger account. A control account or master account is the general ledger account that summarizes the account balances of a subsidiary ledger.
A General Ledger is a collection of numbered accounts that a company uses to keep track of its financial activities and create financial reports. Each account is a distinct record that summarizes a single asset, liability, equity, revenue, or cost.
An Audit Trail is a step-by-step record by which accounting, trade details, or other financial data can be traced to their source. Audit trails are used to verify and track many types of transactions, including accounting transactions and trades in brokerage accounts.
A Digital Audit Trail includes all records that document each stage of a corporate transaction. It contains critical financial data such as invoices and receipts. It also contains contracts, payroll records, and everything else involved with a firm and its activities.
